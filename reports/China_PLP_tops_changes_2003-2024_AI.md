## Structure of the file and notation

The file tracks, for each PLP country and for 2003–2024, the three **top** HS2 chapters in China’s imports (M) and exports (X), their values, and year‑on‑year percentage changes.[1]
Each year is tagged with: “!” (change in one of the top 3 chapters), “≈” (same 3 chapters, different order), or “=” (same 3 and same order), which makes it possible to see how stable the leading products are over time.[1]

## Main patterns in China’s imports (M) from PLPs

Across Angola, Brazil and Mozambique, China’s imports are dominated by natural resources: fuels (27), ores (26), agricultural commodities like oilseeds (12), and, in some cases, wood (44) and metals such as copper (74) or aluminium (76).[1]

On the export side, China consistently sells manufactured products and capital goods: electrical machinery (85), general machinery (84), vehicles (87), textiles and clothing (61, 63, 64), furniture (94), toys (95), and, in some markets, chemicals (29, 38), optics/medical instruments (90) and ships (89).[1]

## Stability vs. volatility in top products

For large partners (Angola, Brazil, Mozambique, Portugal), the top import and export chapters are remarkably stable: after an initial phase with many “!” years, the notation increasingly switches to “≈” or “=”, indicating that the same three chapters remain dominant and only their ranking changes.[1]
Smaller PLPs show much more volatility, with frequent “!” years reflecting small bases and the impact of single large shipments (e.g. new entries of 74 copper in Cabo Verde’s imports, 89 ships in Equatorial Guinea’s and Timor‑Leste’s exports, or 69 ceramics in Guinea‑Bissau’s exports).[1]

## Asymmetry and upgrading

The file highlights a clear asymmetry: China primarily imports commodities and raw materials from the PLPs and exports higher value‑added manufactured goods, a pattern that holds across all partners.[1]
Over time, there is some upgrading and diversification on the Chinese export side (growing roles for 87 vehicles, 90 optical/medical instruments, 89 ships and 95 toys; and for textiles 61/63 in some markets), while the PLPs’ export structures remain heavily commodity‑based with only limited movement into new HS2 chapters.[1]
